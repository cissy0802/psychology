DAY 34 · PSYCHOLOGY OF AWE

Awe & Transcendence: The Moment the "Self" Shrinks

2026.07.07 · BigCat's Inner World
Awe isn't vague poetic feeling—it's a measurable, distinct emotion with a clear neural signature and behavioral effects. Why does that endlessly chattering "I" suddenly go quiet under a night sky, inside a swell of music, or at the sight of a stranger's kindness? The answer isn't mysticism, but a psychological resource you can deliberately draw on.

Awe: Making Room for the VastAwe — Vastness & Accommodation

Psychology of Awe · Emotion
Core Insight

Keltner & Haidt define awe by two features: perceived vastness (something that exceeds your everyday frame of reference—physical, as in a starry sky, but also moral or intellectual) + a need for accommodation (your current mental schemas can't contain it, so you must update your understanding). So awe isn't an intensified form of "happy"—it's a distinct emotion, sitting at the border of wonder and fear.

The Mechanism

Awe activates the parasympathetic nervous system (the vagus nerve), often with goosebumps (piloerection). Behaviorally it does three things: Rudd et al. (2012) found awe expands subjective time—people feel "time is more abundant," and become more patient and more helpful; Stellar et al. (2015) found it linked to lower levels of the inflammatory cytokine IL-6; Piff et al. (2015) showed it directly boosts prosocial behavior and generosity. The shared mechanism: it pulls attention away from "my little concerns" toward "something larger."

The most surprising finding: worldwide, the most common source of awe isn't the night sky or a cathedral, but moral beauty—witnessing others' courage and kindness. Awe is social first.

Applying It
SelfAwe is a resource you can "ration." When depleted, deliberately expose yourself to vast things (stars, grand music, others' good deeds)—it resets your state better than scrolling.
ParentingChildren are naturally awe-prone. Don't rush to fill every wonder with an "explanation"—leave space, ask "what do you think?", protect that "schema-can't-contain-it" process of accommodation.
RelationshipExperiencing awe together (a show, a starry sky) creates "shared vastness"—pulling both of you out of your separate small selves at once.
TeamMaking clear the "bigger picture" the work plugs into (users, mission) evokes an awe-like sense of meaning—a more durable, burnout-resistant driver than KPIs (echoing SDT / Day 17).
Self-check + Common Misconception: treating awe as a synonym for "positivity." Awe has a dark side—facing a vast threat (disaster, a charismatic tyrant) is also awe, and it can make people submit and lose their agency. Awe is a neutral amplifier.

Mini-exercise: recall the last time you truly got goosebumps—which kind of vastness above did it belong to?
Key references · Keltner & Haidt, "Approaching Awe" (2003, Cognition & Emotion) · Dacher Keltner, Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder (2023) · Piff et al., "Awe, the Small Self, and Prosocial Behavior" (2015, JPSP) · Rudd, Vohs & Aaker (2012, Psychological Science)
Insight: "Awe is the feeling of being in the presence of something vast that transcends your current understanding of the world." — Keltner & Haidt.
This Week's Practice + Reflection Build your own "awe list": write down 3 sources that reliably evoke awe in you (a piece of music, a landscape, the story of someone you admire), and keep them as emotional tools you can reach for anytime.

Reflect: does awe have to point "outward" (the vast cosmos)? Can looking inward—at consciousness, cells, the birth of an idea—be equally vast?

The Small Self: Turning Down the "I"The Small Self

Psychology of Awe · Self
Core Insight

Awe's most central effect is to make the "self" feel smaller. Piff et al. call this the "small self"—not low self-worth, but a temporary thinning of the self's boundaries: personal worries, status, and fixations all matter less, replaced by a sense of connection to a larger whole.

The Mechanism

Neurally, self-referential processing is handled largely by the default mode network (DMN)—the inner narrative system that keeps you thinking "how am I doing, what do others think of me" (echoing Day 24). Awe, deep meditation, and psychedelic experiences all correlate with reduced DMN activity (Carhart-Harris). When the DMN quiets, the chattering "I" exits the stage, and subjectively that's the "small self." Bai et al. (2017) replicated "awe → small self" in both US and Chinese samples—so this is not a product of Western individualism.

Applying It
SelfRumination is essentially the DMN stuck in the "I" loop. When you spiral into self-criticism, actively seeking the vast (stepping out to look at the sky, playing grand music) is a way to interrupt rumination physiologically—more effective than "just let it go."
ParentingAdolescents have intense self-consciousness (the "everyone is watching me" imaginary-audience effect). Taking them into larger scales of space and time (nature, history, the cosmos) gently dilutes that excessive self-focus.
RelationshipIn an argument both sides are trapped in "my position." Deliberately zooming the view out to "will this still matter in ten years?" is a technique for summoning the small self and lowering the DMN.
TeamExcessive ego is the biggest source of friction in collaboration. A shared sense of mission shrinks "my credit" down to "our work."
Self-check + Common Misconception: mistaking "small self" for "belittling yourself / self-negation." Quite the opposite—people who experience the small self report stronger connection and greater wellbeing, not a sense of insignificance. The small self is "turning down the volume of the self," not "lowering your self-worth."
Cross-disciplinary echo: Buddhism's "no-self" (anattā) has a genuine intersection with reduced DMN activity and the small self. The dissolution of self-boundaries that seasoned meditators report in deep states shares part of its neural substrate with psychedelic "ego dissolution"—this is an empirical meeting point of current neuroscience and contemplative research (Judson Brewer, Carhart-Harris), not a forced analogy.
Key references · Piff et al. (2015, JPSP) · Bai et al., "Awe, the Diminished Self, and Collective Engagement" (2017, JPSP) · Carhart-Harris et al., DMN & psychedelics · Judson Brewer, The Craving Mind (2017)
Insight: "In the presence of the vast, the self grows quiet." — the small self isn't the self disappearing; it's turning down a narration that's always running.
This Week's Practice + Reflection Next time rumination flares up, don't "analyze" it—instead get up and stand for 5 minutes where you can see the sky or a distant horizon, and notice whether it loosens.

Reflect: if the "I" is largely a narrative manufactured by the DMN, then "letting go of the self"—is it losing something, or just turning down a running commentary?

Self-Transcendent Experiences: An Underrated ContinuumSelf-Transcendent Experiences

Transpersonal Psychology · Consciousness
Core Insight

Awe, flow, deep meditation, mystical experiences, psychedelic experiences—these seemingly different states are grouped by Yaden et al. (2017) into a single continuum: self-transcendent experiences (STEs), whose shared features are a weakened sense of self + a heightened sense of connection. They aren't mysticism, but researchable psychological states with a common neural signature.

The Mechanism

Yaden locates all STEs along two dimensions: an annihilational axis (how much the sense of self weakens) and a relational axis (how much the felt oneness with the outside world increases). Flow sits at the "low-annihilation" end; mystical union at the "high" end. This coordinate system turns a messy pile of "spiritual vocabulary" into a comparable, measurable continuum.

Yaden's Framework: Locating All Transcendent Experiences on Two Axes
Low annihilation · High connectionFlow, deep engagement (Day 18)—the self remains, but merges with what you're doing.
High annihilation · High connectionMystical / union experiences, peak awe—the "I" nearly vanishes, connected to all things.
Low annihilation · Low connectionOrdinary waking focus—the everyday default state.
High annihilation · Low connectionAbsorbed self-forgetting, certain deep meditation—"forgetting yourself," but not necessarily with a sense of oneness.
Historical note: "peak experience" is a concept from Abraham Maslow's 1960s humanistic psychology, based partly on case studies and introspection. But it has not been overturned—rather, Yaden et al. reactivated it with more rigorous methods as measurable STE research: "old concept, new evidence," fundamentally different from the falsified structural theory of Freud.
Applying It
SelfYou need not chase rare mystical experiences. The "mild end" of the spectrum—a deep flow state, a piece of music, a fully absorbed hug—brings the same transcendent benefits, and is available daily.
ParentingDon't treat a child's "spacing out" as all distraction. Absorbed engagement (building blocks and losing track of time) is a healthy seed of self-transcendence, worth protecting.
RelationshipDeeply synced togetherness, facing life-and-death moments together—these are STEs within a relationship, often the "stickiest" anchors in relational memory.
TeamCollective flow (a team's "self-forgetting" state in sport or a crunch project) is a team STE—rare, but it strongly reinforces identity.
Self-check + Common Misconception: romanticizing STEs as "attainable only through psychedelics or extreme practice," or treating them as an exit from reality. Research stresses that their value lies in the aftermath—whether they make you more connected, kinder, more meaning-rich when you return to life. Chasing the experience itself ("spiritual materialism") comes up empty.
Key references · Yaden, Haidt, Hood, Vago & Newberg, "The Varieties of Self-Transcendent Experience" (2017, Review of General Psychology) · Maslow, Toward a Psychology of Being (1962, historical) · Csikszentmihalyi, flow research
Insight: "Self-transcendent experiences are defined by a decreased self-salience and increased feelings of connectedness." — Yaden et al.
This Week's Practice + Reflection Recall 2–3 times in your life when you "forgot who you were, and time disappeared," and place them on Yaden's two axes—which path do you enter through most easily?

Reflect: are flow (Day 18) and mystical experience a difference of degree or of kind? If it's one continuum, is everyday "deep engagement" the most underrated spiritual practice?

Nature, Awe Walks & RestorationNature, Awe Walks & Restoration

Environmental Psychology · Application
Core Insight

Nature is the most accessible, most reliable source of awe. But the key isn't "going into nature"—it's going with an awe-oriented way of attending. Sturm et al.'s (2020) "awe walk" study showed: on the same walk, older adults guided to actively notice the grand and the novel had significantly higher positive and prosocial emotions after 8 weeks—and even the "self" in their selfies got smaller and smaller.

The Mechanism

Kaplan's Attention Restoration Theory (ART) explains why nature restores the mind: cities demand "directed attention" (effortful, depleting), while nature offers "soft fascination" (effortlessly engaging, like flowing water or the play of shadows), letting directed attention rest. Awe adds another layer: it actively turns attention from the self toward the vast. Together—nature restores attention, awe resets the self. Astronauts' "overview effect" (White) is the extreme version: in the instant of seeing Earth from space, borders and conflicts dissolve into a single "fragile blue dot."

Applying It
SelfUpgrade a "walk" into an "awe walk": put the phone away and deliberately look for details you've never noticed (leaf veins, the structure of clouds, shifts of light). Same time, vastly different restorative effect.
ParentingOn outdoor time with a child, a little less "teaching facts," a little more "marveling together." Shared awe builds their relationship with nature better than explanation.
RelationshipA regular "awe date" (watching a sunrise, going into the mountains, live music) is a low-cost, high-return relationship investment—triggering both the small self and shared vastness.
TeamHold your offsite in a grand setting (in nature rather than a meeting room)—using the environment to summon a larger perspective.
Self-check + Common Misconception: assuming "more is better / you must go to distant, spectacular nature." Research shows everyday micro-doses (a tree outside the window, the sky on your commute) are just as effective; what matters is the way of attending, not the grandeur of the scenery. Awe can be in a single flower, or in a galaxy.
Key references · Sturm et al., "Big Smile, Small Self: Awe Walks Promote Prosocial Positive Emotions" (2020, Emotion) · Kaplan, "The Restorative Benefits of Nature" (1995) · Frank White, The Overview Effect (1987)
Insight: "You need not travel far. Awe is available in the everyday—if you go looking for it."
This Week's Practice + Reflection Take one 15-minute "awe walk": no phone, one task only—actively look for something that amazes you, and afterward note the details you'd normally miss.

Reflect: if the key to awe is the "way of attending" rather than "where you go," then what we lack—is it grand scenery, or the habit of looking up?

Questions at the EdgeQuestions at the Edge

1. Is awe's "small self" the same thing as Buddhism's "no-self"?
There's a genuine intersection, but don't equate them. Neurally, both correlate with reduced DMN activity, and subjectively both are a dissolution of self-boundaries. But Buddhism's "no-self" (anattā) is a whole philosophical and contemplative system about the nature of the self—holding that "I" is a dependently-arisen composite with no permanent essence; whereas psychology's "small self" is a single state-based, measurable emotional effect that comes and goes. The small-self effect is like a brief, physiological preview of the "no-self" insight—but equating one awe experience with awakening is mistaking the map for the territory.
2. Why can the same emotion make people more generous, and also be used to manipulate?
Because awe's core is letting the self yield to something larger—and awe itself passes no judgment on what that "larger thing" is. Pointed at nature, moral beauty, a shared mission, the yielding self buys connection and prosociality; steered toward a leader, an ideology, an inciting rally, the same "shrinking of the self" becomes a disarming of critical thinking. So healthy awe needs a question attached: is this vastness inviting me to connect, or demanding that I submit?
3. Is the digital age systematically manufacturing an "awe deficit"?
Very possibly. Awe depends on two things screens are best at destroying: sustained attention and unexpected vastness. Infinite scroll chops attention into fragments, making it hard to dwell on a scene or an idea long enough for "accommodation" to occur; the algorithm-fed "wow" is a calibrated dopamine hit, with the appearance of wonder but without the shrinking of the self. Worse, screens fill every "boring gap"—and it's precisely in those gaps that awe gets its chance to enter. The remedy is counterintuitive: not seeking more spectacular content, but deliberately leaving space, and re-practicing the habit of looking up.
4. As psychedelic research reduces "spirituality" to brain activity, is that disenchantment or deepening?
It depends on how you read "reduce." Finding that mystical experience corresponds to reduced DMN activity and specific serotonin-receptor activation doesn't explain away the experience's meaning—just as knowing love corresponds to oxytocin doesn't cheapen love. Neural correlates address "how it happens," not "what it means." The real value of this research is a bridge: it lets millennia of contemplative insight about consciousness and the self be, for the first time, empirically tested and responsibly applied. So it's more like deepening—what it dispels is superstition; what it leaves is the phenomenon itself.
